Looking at the overall evidence on record, it is difficult D to come to the conclusion that when the appellant struck the deceased with the weapon of offence, he intended to cause such bodily injury as was sufficient in the ordinary course of nature to cause death. The weapon of offence in the present case is a common agriculture tool. If a man is hit with a weed axe on the E head with sufficient force, it is bound to cause, as here, death. It is true that the injuries shown in the post mortem report are fracture of the parietal bone as well as the temporal bone. The deceased died on account of the cerebral compression i.e. internal head injuries. However, the moot question is â€“ whether that by itself is sufficient to draw an inference that the appellant intended F to cause such bodily injury as was sufficient to cause death. This Court is of the view that the appellant could only be attributed with the knowledge that it was likely to cause an injury which was likely to cause the death.
