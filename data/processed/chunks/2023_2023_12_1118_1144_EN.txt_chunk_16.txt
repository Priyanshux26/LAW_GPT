The dismissal of the appeal in the case of Pawan Gupta(supra) without any reasons being recorded would not attract Article 141 of the Constitution of India as no law was declared by the Supreme Court, which will have a binding eff ect on all courts and tribunals in India. There is a clear distinction between the binding law of precedents in terms of Article 141 of the Constitution of India and the doctrine of merger and res judicata. To merge, as held in Kunhayammed (supra), and Khoday Distilleries Ltd. (supra) means to sink or disappear in something else, to become absorbed or extinguished. The logic behind the doctrine of merger is that there cannot be more than one decree or operative s governing the same subject matter at a given point of time. When a decree or passed by an inferior court, tribunal or authority is subjected to a remedy available under law before a superior forum, then the decree or under challenge continues to be eff ective and binding; nevertheless, its fi nality is put in jeopardy. Once the superior court disposes the dispute before it in any manner, either by affi rming the decree or , by setting aside or modifying the same, it is the decree of the superior court, tribunal or authority, which is the fi nal binding and operative decree. The decree and of the inferior court, tribunal or authority gets merged into the passed by the superior forum. However, as has been clarifi ed in both decisions, this doctrine is not of universal or unlimited application.
